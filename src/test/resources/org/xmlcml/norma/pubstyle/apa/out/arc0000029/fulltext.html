The Crisis of Confidence in Research Findings in Psychology: Is Lack of Replication the Real Problem? Or Is It Something Else?

Frank L. Schmidt , In-Sue Oh
  AUTHOR AFFILIATIONS
Frank L. Schmidt Department of Management and Organizations, University of Iowa
In-Sue OhDepartment of Human Resource Management, Fox School of Business, Temple University
© 2016 The Author(s)
  CITATION

Schmidt, F. L., & Oh, I.-S. (2016). The crisis of confidence in research findings in psychology: Is lack of replication the real problem? Or is it something else? Archives of Scientific Psychology, 4(1), 32-37. http://dx.doi.org/10.1037/arc0000029

ABSTRACT

 
Many people have worried that research studies are not repeated or replicated to ensure the results are real. But in most research areas meta-analyses combining across numerous studies on a question have been published. This shows most studies are being replicated. Some argue that if an attempt to replicate a study produces a result that is not statistically significant, this indicates that the first study was in error. This is usually not the case, because many replication studies have only a limited probability of “detecting” a real effect (called low statistical power). This also applies if the initial study does not get statistically significant results—the effect or relation may exist but the study failed to detect it. So even a study like that needs to be replicated. Meta-analyses, which combine the results of multiple studies, can solve these problems but only if the problems of publication bias and questionable research practices are first solved. Publication bias occurs when only studies that “get the ‘right’ results” are published, and questionable research practices distort the research results reported. Both are described in this article.

SCIENTIFIC ABSTRACT

 
There have been frequent expressions of concern over the supposed failure of researchers to conduct replication studies. But the large number of meta-analyses in our literatures shows that replication studies are in fact being conducted in most areas of research. Many who argue for replication as the “gold standard” consider a nonsignificant replication attempt to be strong evidence against the initial study, an interpretation that ignores statistical power, typically low in behavioral research. Many researchers also hold that there is no need to replicate a nonsignificant finding, believing it will always replicate perfectly, an erroneous belief. These beliefs lead to a widely accepted sequential model of the research process that is deficient because it assumes that a single study can answer a research question, a belief that meta-analysis has shown to be false. Meta-analysis can provide the solution to these problems if the problems of publication bias and questionable research practices are successfully addressed. The real problem is not a lack of replication; it is the distortion of our research literatures caused by publication bias and questionable research practices.

KEYWORDS:

replication, meta-analysis, publication bias, questionable research practices

There has recently been considerable angst expressed over the supposed failure of researchers to replicate their research. Some researchers—and even some methodologists—see replication as the solution to the current crisis of confidence in the soundness and trustworthiness of research findings in the literature (cf. Bartlett, 2012; Kepes & McDaniel, 2013, p. 261; Makel, Plucker, & Hegarty, 2012; Novotney, 2014; Pashler & Harris, 2012; Pashler & Wagenmakers, 2012; Ritchie, Wiseman, & French, 2012; Roediger, 2012; Saey, 2015). They see replication as “the gold standard of science” that is missing from our literatures.
We argue that, contrary to common belief: (a) studies in most areas are replicated; (b) failure to replicate a study is usually not evidence against the initial study’s conclusions; (c) an initial study with a nonsignificant finding requires replication; (d) a single study can never answer a scientific question; (e) the widely used sequential study research program model does not work; and (f) randomization does not work when sample sizes are small. To our knowledge, most of these arguments are new in the literature.
We argue further that attainment of reliable cumulative knowledge requires meta-analysis of multiple replications, preferably conceptual replications, and that the major threat to cumulative knowledge is not failure to replicate but rather widespread questionable research practices (QRPs), especially in experimental areas. Our position is that the current obsession with replication is a red herring distracting attention from the real threats to the validity of cumulative knowledge in the behavioral sciences.
Some commentators argue that most psychological studies are never replicated. Yet the literature is full of meta-analyses, and most are based on a substantial number of studies (e.g., see reviews by Lipsey & Wilson, 1993, and Richard, Bond, & Stokes-Zoota, 2003). Meta-analyses by definition are based on multiple replications. So obviously there are many replications in many areas of the behavioral and social sciences. Psychological Bulletin contains many of these meta-analyses but so do many other journals (Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, Ch. 1; Schmidt, Oh, & Hayes, 2009). As pointed out by Aronson, Ellsworth, and Gonzales (1990), there are different types of replication, all of which are useful: (a) literal replication—the same researcher conducts a new study in exactly the same way as in the original study; (b) operational replication—a different researcher attempts to duplicate the original study using exactly the same procedures (also called direct replication); and (c) systematic replication—a different researcher conducts a study in which many features of the original study are maintained but some aspects (e.g., type of subjects or measures used) are changed (also called conceptual replication). Systematic replications are more common and also more informative than literal and operational replications, because they test the generalizability of findings across different types of subjects, measures, and so on. From a triangulation perspective, if findings across different types of subjects, measures, design, and so forth lead to the same conclusion, that increases confidence in generalizability (Sackett & Larson, 1990). As Eden (2002) has said, “the less similar a replication is to an original study, the greater its potential contribution” (p. 842; italics in original). For this reason, systematic replications contribute the most to cumulative knowledge and to meta-analysis. For example, if the meta-analysis shows that all the variation in findings across varied systematic (conceptual) replications is due to sampling error and other research artifacts, the conclusion is that none of the many differences between these studies has an effect on the relationship in question. This means the relation in question is always the same, a powerful conclusion. If all but a small amount of variation is explained by research artifacts, the conclusion is that most of the study differences have little effect and the relation in question varies little. A single large study with sample size equal to the sum of the study sample sizes in such a meta-analysis is not capable of revealing these facts (Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, pp. 372–374). Although systematic or conceptual replications are most informative, all replications can contribute to the meta-analyses needed to attain cumulative knowledge.

Errors in Thinking About Replication

 
Commentators advocating replication as the gold standard of science typically assume that a replication should be interpreted in a stand-alone manner. For example, if an initial study finding a significant result is followed up by an attempt at replication that fails to obtain a statistically significant result, this “failure to replicate” is assumed to constitute important scientific evidence casting doubt on the initial significant finding. In this way of thinking, failure to replicate is defined as failure to get a significant result, and successful replication is defined as getting a significant result. As an example, an article in the October 2013 issue of the Association for Psychological Sciences’ (APS) Observer discussed as a serious replication failure a study with a total N of only 40 that failed to obtain significance. This way of thinking is defective, because it ignores the role of statistical power. Average statistical power in most psychological literatures ranges from .40 to .50. (cf. Cohen, 1962, 1977, 1988, 1992; Bakker et al., 2012; Schmidt, Hunter, & Urry, 1976), and has not increased over the last 50 years (Sedlmeier & Gigerenzer, 1989; Maxwell, 2004; Shen, Kiger, Davies, Rasch, Simon, & Ones, 2011). Statistical power is critically important when an effect or relation exists that the study might fail to detect. How often do such effects or relations exist? There is evidence is that the null is almost never true in many areas of research. For example, Lipsey and Wilson (1993) examined meta-analyses of over 300 psychological interventions. In only one of these interventions was the null hypothesis true (less than 1%). Based on 322 meta-analyses of social psychological phenomena, Richard, Bond, and Stokes-Zoota (2003) found that this is also true in many areas of social psychology.
In some areas of research the null may be true or essentially true more frequently, but in such areas statistical power is still critical. Without high statistical power, a study cannot distinguish between a true null hypothesis and a Type II error (failure to detect a real relationship). Note that if confidence intervals (CIs) were used instead of significance tests, there would be far fewer “failures to replicate”—because the CIs would often overlap, indicating no conflict between the two studies (Borenstein, 1994; Cumming, 2012; Schmidt, 1992, 1996). Beginning early in 2014, the journal Psychological Science started requiring use of CIs, an important and praiseworthy development (Association for Psychological Science, 2013).
A typical level of power means that a failure to replicate tells you just about nothing. Research in meta-analysis has shown that no single study can answer any question, because too many factors perturb the results of any given study (cf. Schmidt & Hunter, 2015). Sampling error is the most important factor. Sampling error is the difference between an estimate of a population parameter and the actual value of the population parameter that the sample is intended to estimate. Researchers greatly underestimate the size of sampling errors, as shown by the Schmidt, Ocasio, Hillery, and Hunter (1985) study. The data set from a prior study based on 1,455 subjects was available to these authors; because of the large sample the relationship in question was accurately estimated (at r = .22). They then divided those data randomly into smaller nonoverlapping studies (N = 30 and N = 68). These small N studies produced widely varying estimates (rs ranging from −.21 to +.61), illustrating the huge effect of sampling errors. We found that when researchers were shown these findings they expressed astonishment, stating that they could not believe that sampling errors alone could produce such extreme variation in results.
Because most researchers do not realize how large sampling errors can be, they do not appreciate how much capitalization on sampling error can inflate research findings. An example of this would be selecting only the largest correlations from the small N studies described here for inclusion in a research report. Many researchers believe in the false law of small numbers. They believe that a random sample is representative of the population from which it is drawn, even if that sample is small. In fact, it is representative only if the random sample is large. Because of sampling error, small samples are randomly unrepresentative, as demonstrated by Tversky and Kahneman (1971). This is a major problem in small sample randomized experiments, as discussed later. So even if significance tests were not used, and CIs were used instead, this would still be a problem in many experiments. CIs are based on sampling error, so the CIs would be very wide, indicating the study contains little information, as illustrated in Schmidt (1992). Other distorting factors beyond sampling error include measurement error, range variation, imperfect construct validity of measures, artificial dichotomization of continuous measures, and others (cf. Schmidt & Hunter, 2015).
These considerations mean that the purpose of replication studies is to provide the studies needed for a meta-analysis, because “meta-analysis is not possible unless the needed primary studies are conducted” (Eden, 2002, p. 842). Meta-analysis of multiple studies solves the problem of low statistical power and precision in individual studies. CIs in meta-analysis are typically quite narrow. Even if none of the studies going into a meta-analysis reports statistically significant results, meta-analysis can still reveal that there is an important relationship. Nonsignificance in underpowered studies is not informative.
A telling example of this comes from the study of the effect of highway shoulders on automobile accidents and deaths (Hauer, 2004). A number of studies had been conducted to determine whether highway shoulders, which allow drivers to pull over to the side of the road and stop if they need to, reduce accidents and deaths. None of these inadequately powered studies found a statistically significant relationship between the presence or absence of shoulders and accidents or deaths. Traffic safety engineers concluded that shoulders have no effect, and as a result fewer shoulders were built in most states. Hauer’s (2004) meta-analysis of these studies showed clearly that shoulders reduced both accidents and deaths. In this case, people died as a result of failure to understand sampling error and statistical power.
Another example is the case of the relation between the personality trait of conscientiousness and job performance. The studies in that literature were based on small Ns and statistical power in individual studies was typically less than .20. So the vast majority of studies obtained and reported nonsignificant results and the conclusion was that conscientiousness was unrelated to job performance. Yet meta-analysis based on Euro American studies showed that the average relationship was .23, with little variation around this value (cf. Schmidt, Oh, & Shaffer, 2008). Later meta-analyses in each of four East Asian countries showed that, after accounting for second order sampling error across these countries, this value was also .23 in each of those countries, illustrating how meta-analysis can yield successful replication of findings when individual studies cannot (Oh, 2009; Schmidt & Oh, 2013) and thereby can reveal cumulative knowledge. These two examples also clearly illustrate that the solution is not replications, but systematic integration of existing replications via meta-analysis.

Replicability of Nonsignificant Findings

 
What about initially nonsignificant findings? A nonsignificant finding is usually interpreted as indicating the absence of a relationship. A commonly heard expression is: “If it is not significant, it is zero” (see statements of this sort summarized in Schmidt & Hunter, 1997). This is unjustified, because it is very possible, even likely, that the relationship exists but was not detected due to low power. What about the replicability of nonsignificant findings? It is usually assumed that nonsignificant findings do not need replication, on grounds that a nonsignificant finding by definition will replicate perfectly, because such a finding is believed to demonstrate the absence of a relationship. But if a nonsignificant finding is due to low power—which is very likely—it will not replicate any better than a significant finding. In both cases, the typical probability of replication is 40% to 50%.
Hence we have a situation where an initially nonsignificant finding will not be followed up with additional studies, even though it should be. These facts point up Ronald Fisher’s error, as discussed in Schmidt and Hunter (1997). Fisher made his error because he denied the concept of statistical power. For him, there was no Type II error, only Type I error. The concept of statistical power was introduced later by Neyman and Pearson and was never accepted by Fisher. Fisher endorsed the erroneous concept of replication discussed earlier. He said a relation is real only if it consistently replicates itself, that is, shows consistent significant findings (Schmidt & Hunter, 1997). A relationship may be real if it shows inconsistent significant findings. In fact, given typical levels of statistical power, a relation that shows consistent nonsignificant findings may be real. For example, if power is .50, the probability of three independent nonsignificant findings in a row is (.50)(.50)(.50) = .12. If power is .40, this probability is .22; that is, (.60)(.60)(.60). Hence three studies all showing nonsignificant results cannot confidently be interpreted as indicating the absence of a relationship given the non-negligible possibility (.12 or .22) that there exists a relation.
Richard et al. (2003) found that the average effect size in social psychology is d = .40 (corresponding to r = .20) based on more than 300 meta-analyses. Bakker et al. (2012) summarized the research showing that this is approximately true in psychology experiments in other research areas. Bakker et al. also showed that the median sample size in psychological experiments is only 40. The relevant figures for laboratory experiments in other areas, such as management, behavioral marketing, and behavioral economics are very likely to be similar. Given typical effects sizes and typical Ns, and the resulting typical levels of statistical power, then in the absence of publication bias and questionable research practices, it is clear that about half of all studies will report significant findings and half will report nonsignificant findings. This sort of situation is usually viewed as indicating a hopelessly conflicting literature. But this is often not the pattern of findings we see reported in the published literature—and therein lies a serious problem that we discuss later.
So what is the solution to the problem of reliable cumulative knowledge from research? It is what advocates of meta-analysts have been saying for years: Multiple studies should be conducted on each hypothesis or relation, and there should be no attempt to draw conclusions from any such individual study in isolation, because meta-analysis has shown that no single study can answer any scientific question. Instead, the meaning of the body of studies is revealed in the results of a meta-analysis. Meta-analysis provides an effective solution to the problems of sampling error and low power and precision in individual studies (Borenstein et al., 2009; Braver et al., 2014; Hedges & Olkin, 1985; Hunter, Schmidt, & Jackson, 1982; Schmidt & Hunter, 2015).

A Deficient Research Process Model

 
The model of cumulative scientific knowledge that many researchers appear to follow today is defective. It is a sequential model, and the steps in the model are as follows. First, the researcher conducts a study that gets significant results for his or her initial hypothesis. He or she then says, “Well, that question has been answered. Now we move on to the next question and test a different hypothesis from this theory.” If the result from the test of this next hypothesis is nonsignificant, the researcher concludes that that hypothesis is disconfirmed and then moves on to another hypothesis. If that result is significant, he or she concludes that the hypothesis is confirmed and moves on to another hypothesis. Following this sequence is often viewed as constituting a “research program.” This model makes the false assumption that a single study can answer a question or establish or disconfirm a hypothesis. But this can only be achieved via a meta-analysis of multiple studies on the same relationship. Sampling error and other research artifacts cause single studies to have statistical power too low to accomplish this.

Biases in the Published Research Literature

 
Leaving aside outright research fraud (Fountain, 2014; Matlack, 2013; Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, pp. 521–523; Verfaellie, 2011), publication bias is an important issue in many research literatures. Publication bias (also called availability or source bias) occurs when published studies (i.e., available studies) are an unrepresentative or biased sample of all existing studies. Publication bias can result from researchers’ decisions about what to submit to journals or from editors’ decisions about what to accept for publication. There is now a very large literature on publication bias (cf. Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, pp. 513–552); space prevents a review of that literature here. The relevant fact here is that publication bias can distort the results of meta-analyses. But this does not mean that meta-analysis methods are flawed. Publication bias is a problem in (some) research literatures, not a problem in meta-analysis methods. Publication bias existed long before meta-analysis appeared on the scene. In conducting a meta-analysis, the appropriate procedures should be applied to detect evidence of publication bias or availability bias. If such evidence is detected, the appropriate adjustment should be made and sensitivity analyses should be reported (e.g., see Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, Ch. 13). A conclusion of publication bias is justified if the sensitivity analysis reveals that different methods for detecting publication bias all indicate the presence of such bias (Kepes, Banks, & Oh, 2014). Both the original results and the results obtained with the adjustment for publication bias should be reported.
In addition to publication bias, there are the biasing effects of QRPs, as shown in recent articles by John et al. (2012), O’Boyle, Banks, and Gonzalez-Mule (in press), and Simmons et al. (2011; cf. Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, pp. 523–530 for a discussion). Examples include (a) adding subjects one by one until the result is significant, then stopping; (b) dropping studies or measures that are not significant; (c) conducting multiple significance tests on a relation and reporting only those that show significance (cherry picking); (d) deciding whether to include data after looking to see the effect on statistical significance; (e) hypothesizing after the results are known (harking); and (f) running a lab experiment over until you get the “right” results. (The first author discovered this being done while serving on promotion and tenure committees.) John et al. (2012) found that large percentages of the researchers they surveyed admitted to using these practices.
The problems of questionable research practices seem to be most severe in laboratory experimental studies. This includes experimental social psychology, general experimental psychology, and lab experiments in management, behavioral marketing, behavioral economics, and behavioral accounting. The findings of Sterling and his associates suggest this (Sterling, 1959; Sterling et al., 1995; Schmidt & Hunter, 2015, pp. 523–525). John et al. (2012) found the rate of admission of using QRPs was highest in social psychology (40%). When classified by “research type,” admission rates were highest in the categories labeled “Laboratory” and “Experimental”—36% in both these areas. John et al. (2012) also found that rated approval of questionable research practices was higher in experimental social psychology than in all other areas except one.

Limitations of Random Assignment

 
The aforementioned findings seem ironic in light of claims of epistemological superiority for experimental studies over nonexperimental studies, based on the assumed power of random assignment to allow inferences of causality. In fact, randomization does not work unless the random samples are large—and they rarely are. Typically, there are about 20 subjects in the experimental group and 20 in the control group (Bakker et al., 2012). Such small randomized sample sizes produce neither equivalent groups nor groups representative of the population of interest, as shown by Tversky and Kahneman (1971). The belief that they do is belief in the law of small numbers. The law of large numbers correctly states that large random samples are representative of the population. The law of small numbers is false; it states that small random samples are representative of the population (Tversky & Kahneman, 1971). Because they are not, they do not provide a basis for inferences of causality.
It may be that research findings from laboratory experiments are the most questionable of any research areas. In this connection, three recent articles have argued that experimental social psychology is in a state of crisis (Bartlett, 2012; Jost, 2013; McGuire, 2013). Related to this is the fact that the organizations now conducting replication studies—such as that of Brian Nosek at the University of Virginia (Center for Open Science; Nosek et al., 2012) and the APS effort in the journal Perspectives on Psychological Science—are all focused on replication of experiments—mostly in social psychology. (The goal of these efforts is to determine whether such studies can be replicated.) It appears this is where the problems are most severe. Lab based experimental studies may be the area in which replication studies have traditionally been less common, with the result being fewer meta-analyses appearing in those areas.

A New Approach to Detecting Questionable Research Practices

 
The frequency of statistical significance in some literatures is suspiciously high given the level of statistical power in the component studies, as shown by Francis (2012a, 2012b, 2013, 2014), Francis et al. (2014), and Schimmack (2012). For example, if 90% of studies report significance when the average power in the set of studies is .40, this is strong evidence of questionable research practices. In the absence of QRPs this outcome has a very low probability. Yet it is found with some frequency in the literature. Power analysis of this sort may turn out to be one of the best methods of detecting untrustworthy research literatures.
As noted earlier, power has not increased in psychological research literatures since Cohen first pointed out the problem of low power in 1962. The question is why? Low statistical power leads to nonsignificant results, which are hard to publish, so researchers have a strong incentive to increase power, as pointed out by Maxwell (2004). But they have not. In a 1992 article in Current Directions in Psychological Science, Jacob Cohen puzzled over the reason for this. Is it possible that the reason is that researchers have found a way to avoid the high frequency of nonsignificant findings that are a logical consequence of low statistical power? The way to avoid this consequence is to use questionable research practices that create statistical significance where it does not actually exist. Bakker et al. (2012; see also O’Boyle et al., in press) concluded this was likely the case, as did Schmidt and Hunter (2015, pp. 526–530).
Questionable research practices and publication bias distort the results of meta-analyses, and this effect has been demonstrated by Bakker et al. (2012) and Levine, Asada, and Carpenter (2009), among others. The usual effect is an upward bias in mean effect sizes and a downward bias in the variability across effect sizes due to the unavailability of low effect-size studies. So in many areas of research, perhaps most, the real threat to cumulative knowledge is not failure of studies to replicate when replication is attempted. Nor is it a failure of researchers to attempt replication. The fact that we have so many meta-analyses in our literatures proves that replication studies are carried out in most areas of research. The real threat is distortion of available research literatures caused by questionable research practices and publication bias. These are the severe problems we need to address. These problems must be addressed because they threaten the accuracy of the results of meta-analyses, and meta-analyses are the only real foundation for cumulative knowledge.1Footnote 1
A reviewer questioned the statement that meta-analysis is the only real foundation for cumulative knowledge, saying that a single well conducted large sample study, such as a national probability sample, can provide cumulative knowledge without the need for meta-analysis. This objection is addressed in detail in Schmidt and Hunter (2015, pp. 372–374). As noted earlier in this article, a meta-analysis of a large number of smaller studies that are conceptual replications allows the researchers to determine whether the differences in outcomes across the studies are explained by statistical artifacts such as sampling error variance and differences in levels of measurement error. If this is found to be the case, there is a powerful conclusion that study differences do not moderate study outcomes and that the relationship is constant across studies. If the variation in study outcomes cannot be fully explained by statistical artifacts, then meta-analysis allows the researcher to test for study characteristics that moderate study outcomes. A single large sample study, no matter how well conducted, cannot yield either of these items of information, both of which are critical to reliable cumulative knowledge. Finally, if multiple large sample studies eventually appear, then meta-analysis is still necessary to combine across these studies to reach the most accurate estimate of the meaning of the set of studies as a whole.

    A reviewer questioned the statement that meta-analysis is the only real foundation for cumulative knowledge, saying that a single well conducted large sample study, such as a national probability sample, can provide cumulative knowledge without the need for meta-analysis. This objection is addressed in detail in Schmidt and Hunter (2015, pp. 372–374). As noted earlier in this article, a meta-analysis of a large number of smaller studies that are conceptual replications allows the researchers to determine whether the differences in outcomes across the studies are explained by statistical artifacts such as sampling error variance and differences in levels of measurement error. If this is found to be the case, there is a powerful conclusion that study differences do not moderate study outcomes and that the relationship is constant across studies. If the variation in study outcomes cannot be fully explained by statistical artifacts, then meta-analysis allows the researcher to test for study characteristics that moderate study outcomes. A single large sample study, no matter how well conducted, cannot yield either of these items of information, both of which are critical to reliable cumulative knowledge. Finally, if multiple large sample studies eventually appear, then meta-analysis is still necessary to combine across these studies to reach the most accurate estimate of the meaning of the set of studies as a whole.
